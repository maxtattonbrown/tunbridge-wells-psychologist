World Mental Health Day: Practical Tips for Taking Care of Your Mental Wellbeing

10 Oct
Written By Rachel Whatmough

Every year, on October 10th, the world comes together to mark World Mental Health Day. It's an opportunity to raise awareness, spark important conversations, and encourage action for better mental health for everyone. This year, the focus is on mental health as a universal human right—something that every person deserves access to, regardless of where they live, their age, or their circumstances.

For those of us living in the UK, the pressures of modern life can often feel overwhelming. Whether it's managing work-life balance, navigating family responsibilities, or simply coping with the everyday challenges that come our way, maintaining our mental health is just as important as taking care of our physical health. As a clinical psychologist, I want to offer practical tips and insights that can help everyone—whether you're seeking to enhance your well-being or support someone you care about.

Understanding Mental Health: It’s More Than the Absence of Illness

Mental health is not just the absence of a mental health condition like anxiety, depression. It's about how we think, feel, and behave in our day-to-day lives. Good mental health allows us to cope with stress, work productively, enjoy fulfilling relationships, and make meaningful contributions to our communities.

We all have mental health, and it exists on a spectrum. You may not have a diagnosed condition, but you could still be experiencing poor mental health due to stress, burnout, or emotional difficulties. Recognising this is important because mental health is something that needs regular attention, just like physical health.

Recognise the Signs of Struggle

In the UK, many of us are good at keeping a stiff upper lip and powering through difficulties, but this can sometimes mask underlying struggles. It’s crucial to recognise when we might be struggling with our mental health. Here are some common signs:

  • Changes in mood: Persistent sadness, irritability, or feeling emotionally overwhelmed.

  • Loss of interest: No longer finding pleasure in activities that you usually enjoy.

  • Fatigue or difficulty sleeping: Constant tiredness, trouble falling asleep, or waking up throughout the night.

  • Difficulty concentrating: Finding it hard to focus, make decisions, or remember things.

  • Physical symptoms: Headaches, digestive issues, or chronic pain that can’t be explained by medical conditions.

These signs don’t necessarily mean you have a mental health disorder, but they are indicators that it might be time to pay attention to your mental well-being and take proactive steps to support yourself.

Practical Tips for Supporting Your Mental Health

There are several evidence-based practices you can incorporate into your daily life to promote good mental health. Here are some practical steps to consider:

1. Prioritise Self-Care

Taking time for self-care is essential. This doesn’t mean you have to schedule elaborate spa days. Self-care can be as simple as taking a walk in nature, reading a book, or practising mindfulness exercises. The key is to carve out time in your day for activities that help you relax and recharge.

  • Exercise: Physical activity is one of the best ways to improve mental health. Whether it's a gentle yoga session, a brisk walk, or a full workout, exercise can lift your mood, reduce stress, and help you sleep better.

  • Sleep: Ensuring you get enough sleep is crucial for emotional regulation and overall well-being. If you struggle with sleep, try establishing a bedtime routine, reducing screen time before bed, and creating a calming sleep environment.

  • Nutrition: What you eat affects your mental health. Eating a balanced diet with plenty of fruits, vegetables, and omega-3 fatty acids can support brain function and improve mood.

2. Set Healthy Boundaries

In our connected world, it can be hard to set boundaries, especially with work or social media. Protecting your mental health might mean setting limits on when you're available for work or taking breaks from social media to avoid information overload and comparison. Learning to say “no” when you're overwhelmed is an important skill that can help protect your mental space.

3. Talk About Your Feelings

It can be difficult to open up about our emotions, but talking about how we feel is one of the most effective ways to manage mental health. Whether you confide in a trusted friend, family member, or mental health professional, expressing your feelings can help alleviate emotional pressure.

4. Practice Mindfulness and Relaxation Techniques

Mindfulness is about being present in the moment and accepting it without judgment. There are many ways to practice mindfulness, from deep breathing exercises to guided meditation. These practices have been shown to reduce stress and improve mental clarity.

Try starting with just a few minutes each day, focusing on your breath or observing your thoughts without trying to change them. Over time, mindfulness can help you manage difficult emotions more effectively.

Supporting Others

Mental health is a collective responsibility. Being aware of the signs that someone else may be struggling can make a significant difference. If someone close to you seems to be having a hard time, offer a listening ear without judgment. Encourage them to seek professional help if necessary, but remember that just being there for them can be a lifeline.

If you're not sure how to help, simply saying, “I’m here for you” or “I care about you” can open the door to meaningful conversations.

When to Seek Professional Help

There are times when self-care strategies may not be enough, and professional support is required. If you or someone you know is experiencing persistent feelings of sadness, anxiety, or hopelessness that interfere with daily life, it’s important to seek help from a mental health professional. 

Final Thoughts

On this World Mental Health Day, let’s remember that mental health is just as important as physical health. Whether you’re looking after your own mental well-being or supporting someone else, taking small, practical steps can make a big difference. Above all, let's work towards a world where mental health is seen not just as a luxury, but as a right that everyone is entitled to.

If you're struggling, remember that help is available—and you don’t have to face it alone.
